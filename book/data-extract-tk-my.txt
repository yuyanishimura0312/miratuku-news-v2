### 1.1 FIFTEEN GTA SELECTIVE CODES (Full Content)
========================================================================

The following 15 core analytical codes derive from Grounded Theory Analysis of
2,184 indigenous groups and 34,459 knowledge items, representing the analytical
structure through which traditional knowledge systems are organized:

[CODE 31] SUBSISTENCE PRIMACY & KNOWLEDGE STRATIFICATION
Indigenous knowledge systems are fundamentally organized around dominant subsistence 
modes, which create epistemological hierarchies. The subsistence mode achieving 
economic primacy (agriculture, pastoralism, maritime, foraging) generates the deepest 
and most formalized knowledge domains. Secondary and supplementary practices are 
systematically underrepresented in both cultural transmission and external documentation.
This stratification actively reproduces itself through differential transmission investment,
creating self-reinforcing knowledge monopolies indexed to subsistence primacy.

[CODE 32] KINSHIP STRUCTURE & KNOWLEDGE TRANSMISSION PATHWAYS
Kinship organization — encompassing descent principles, marriage rules, and post-marital 
residence patterns — constitutes the primary social infrastructure through which indigenous 
knowledge is routed, contained, and reproduced across generations. Patrilineal, matrilineal, 
and bilateral systems each generate distinct transmission pathways that determine who has 
legitimate access to specialized knowledge. The intersection of kinship with craft 
specialization and status inheritance produces bounded knowledge territories that are 
simultaneously social, spatial, and epistemological.

[CODE 33] CRAFT KNOWLEDGE LOCALIZATION & MONOPOLY FORMATION
Specialized craft knowledge (pottery, weaving, leather working, metalwork, maritime craft 
construction) becomes systematically localized within specific kin groups, households, or 
communities through kinship-based apprenticeship, residence rules, and gendered divisions 
of labor. This localization produces knowledge monopolies maintained intergenerationally 
through restricted apprenticeship access. The stability of these localized knowledge systems 
depends on continuity of the social structures — particularly patrilineal or matrilineal 
residence arrangements — that organize knowledge transmission.

[CODE 34] DOCUMENTATION BIAS & KNOWLEDGE INVISIBILITY
Systematic gaps in indigenous traditional knowledge documentation reflect structural biases 
in ethnographic capture that prioritize visible, material, and economically dominant knowledge 
domains over ritual, political, kinship, and supplementary subsistence knowledge. Colonial-era 
and post-colonial documentation practices created self-reproducing lacunae by focusing on 
dominant subsistence modes, male knowledge holders, and externally legible social structures. 
These documentation absences create a paradox in which knowledge systems appear simpler than 
they are.

[CODE 35] ECOLOGICAL ADAPTATION & SUBSISTENCE KNOWLEDGE CALIBRATION
Indigenous knowledge systems are calibrated to specific ecological conditions through 
differential investment in subsistence knowledge domains. Environmental constraints 
(carrying capacity, seasonality, soil type, water availability, terrain) determine which 
knowledge receives formalization, deep transmission investment, and intergenerational stability. 
Mixed subsistence economies in ecologically heterogeneous environments generate more cognitively 
complex and integrative knowledge systems.

[CODE 36] PASTORAL-AGRICULTURAL KNOWLEDGE & ASYMMETRIC INTEGRATION
In mixed agro-pastoral economies, animal husbandry knowledge is systematically integrated 
asymmetrically relative to its actual subsistence contribution, with agricultural knowledge 
achieving primacy while pastoral and animal-related knowledge remains peripheral. This 
asymmetry reflects both ecological constraints and cultural ideological structures that embed 
agricultural knowledge as foundational. The selective underutilization of domesticated animals 
reveals that knowledge integration is an active cultural process.

[CODE 37] MARITIME KNOWLEDGE STANDARDIZATION & SINGULARITY
Maritime and aquatic knowledge systems exhibit distinctive organizational patterns of high 
standardization and low variation, driven by ecological constraints that impose non-negotiable 
technical requirements on watercraft construction, navigation, and resource extraction. The 
safety criticality and technical complexity of maritime activities generate invariant 
transmission protocols and typological rigidity that resist variation typical of terrestrial 
subsistence systems.

[CODE 38] STATUS STRATIFICATION & KNOWLEDGE ACCESS CONTROL
Social stratification systems — encompassing hereditary rank, slavery, occupational 
specialization, and achieved versus ascribed status — function as knowledge access control 
mechanisms that determine who can learn, hold, and transmit specific knowledge domains. 
Hereditary status systems generate the most rigid knowledge monopolies, where elite lineages 
control politically, ritually, and technically sensitive knowledge and commoner access is 
systematically restricted.

[CODE 39] SETTLEMENT PATTERN & KNOWLEDGE SPATIAL ORGANIZATION
Settlement organization — whether dispersed, nucleated, maritime, or neolocal — structures 
the spatial distribution of knowledge across communities by determining which practitioners 
achieve co-residential proximity for observation-based apprenticeship. Dispersed settlement 
patterns produce knowledge fragmentation, while nucleated settlements enable knowledge 
consolidation, standardization, and the emergence of community-level expertise norms.

[CODE 40] AGRICULTURAL TECHNOLOGY & KNOWLEDGE INTENSIFICATION NEXUS
Agricultural technology choices — plow technology, irrigation, fallow management systems — 
are deeply embedded in knowledge systems that reflect cumulative adaptive learning about 
soil fertility, labor organization, and land-use sustainability. The structural absence of 
plow technology in many indigenous agricultural systems represents not a knowledge deficit 
but an ecological and social adaptation that generates distinctive expertise in hand 
cultivation, intercropping, and cyclical land management.

[CODE 41] COUSIN MARRIAGE & ALLIANCE-BASED KNOWLEDGE NETWORKS
Cousin marriage preferences and prescriptive alliance rules structure inter-community knowledge 
exchange networks through which specialized knowledge circulates beyond primary kin groups. 
Cross-cousin marriage systems generate systematic knowledge exchange corridors between allied 
lineages. Affinal relationships created through marriage serve as the primary institutional 
mechanism for controlled knowledge transfer across social boundaries.

[CODE 42] INSTITUTIONAL HERITAGE & KNOWLEDGE FORMALIZATION
Formal institutional mechanisms for heritage recognition — including legal frameworks, UNESCO 
conventions, and national policy instruments — represent a qualitatively distinct knowledge 
formalization pathway that operates alongside traditional kinship-based transmission systems. 
These institutional frameworks legitimize indigenous knowledge by translating it into externally 
legible formats while simultaneously risking decontextualization and freezing of inherently 
dynamic and adaptive knowledge systems.

[CODE 43] MIXED ECONOMY & KNOWLEDGE COGNITIVE FLEXIBILITY
Communities operating mixed subsistence economies develop distinctive cognitive capacities for 
integrating knowledge across multiple resource domains. The integration of complementary 
subsistence modes (agriculture, pastoralism, foraging, maritime) generates knowledge systems 
with built-in redundancy and substitutability, making them more resilient to ecological 
disruption than highly specialized monoculture knowledge systems.

[CODE 44] GENDERED KNOWLEDGE DOMAIN SEGREGATION
Gender constitutes a fundamental axis of knowledge organization, structuring which knowledge 
domains are accessible, transmissible, and authoritative for differently positioned social 
actors. The association of specific craft materials and processes with gender categories 
reflects historically sedimented social arrangements that concentrate expertise within gendered 
social networks. Post-marital residence patterns intersect with gender to create gendered 
knowledge flows.

[CODE 45] ORAL & INTIMATE TRANSMISSION & KNOWLEDGE ENCLOSURE
The predominance of oral, observation-based, and intimate-scale transmission in indigenous 
knowledge systems creates a structural enclosure that maintains knowledge within small kinship 
circles while ensuring high contextual fidelity and practical efficacy of transmitted knowledge. 
This enclosure is not merely a technological limitation but a deliberate epistemological strategy 
that ties knowledge validity to social relationship and lived context. The intimacy of 
transmission ensures quality control embedded in social relationships.

========================================================================

mythological narratives through functional equivalence analysis.

Structure:
- tk_item_id: INTEGER (references tk_items)
- myth_id: INTEGER (references myths in myth_functions.db)
- function_code: TEXT (MM = Myth-Metaphor, ritual, etc.)
- resonance_score: REAL (0.0-1.0 confidence)
- bridge_rationale: TEXT (analytical explanation)

## PART 3: SYNTHESIS FOR BOOK PROJECT
=============================================================================

### 3.1 TRADITIONAL KNOWLEDGE AS BACKWARD-LOOKING SYSTEM
Based on GTA analysis of TK DB:

The 15 selective codes reveal TK is fundamentally organized around:
1. Subsistence primacy (what keeps people alive NOW determines what knowledge matters)
2. Kinship transmission (knowledge moves through existing social relationships)
3. Localization/enclosure (knowledge is kept close, not broadcast)
4. Craft monopoly (expertise is concentrated, not distributed)
5. Oral intimacy (knowledge is experiential, not abstracted)

→ These structures are optimized for STABILITY & LOCAL COHERENCE, not
  for anticipatory knowledge of distant futures. TK is tuned to ecological
  cycles and life course patterns, not long-term structural change.

### 3.2 MYTHS AS FUTURE-ENCODING DEVICES
Based on myth function analysis:

Despite TK being backward-looking, myths function as compensatory system:
- 27,713 narrative function myths = cultural container for transmitting knowledge
  through engaging story form (entertainment as pedagogy)
- 13,421 psychological healing myths = emotional preparation for uncertainty
  and loss
- 11,454 worldview myths = cosmological frameworks that normalize change
- 8+ prophecy archetypes = explicit future-oriented narrative patterns

→ Myths function as the cultural mechanism through which societies encode
  anticipated futures, contingencies, and transformations that TK systems
  cannot fully address.

### 3.3 KNOWLEDGE DOMAIN GAPS (FUTURE-ORIENTED KNOWLEDGE ABSENT)
Zero-count domains in TK DB suggest systematic documentation/transmission gaps:
- Weather & Climate Knowledge (0 items)
- Celestial Navigation (0)  
- Territory Knowledge (0)
- Mythology & Cosmology (0)
- Water Management (0)
- Fire Management (0)

These are precisely the knowledge domains most relevant to:
- Prediction (weather, climate)
- Navigation (celestial bodies, territory)
- Long-term sustainability (water, fire, resource management)

→ The database reflects the paradox: the knowledge systems themselves may
  contain sophisticated future-oriented knowledge, but the transmission
  structures and documentation practices suppress their visibility.

### 3.4 BRIDGE DATA IMPLICATIONS
Myth-Function bridge: Highest resonance between myths and TK domains focused
on oral transmission, performance, and identity.

Anthropology bridge: Highest confidence (0.56) between TK and anthropological
concepts focused on kinship alliance and cousin marriage — systems that are
explicitly inter-group and future-oriented (alliance rules determine genetic
futures across generations).

→ The bridges suggest myths and formal kinship structures serve related
  functions: both are mechanisms for transmitting information about social
  futures across generational boundaries.

## APPENDIX: DATABASE QUERY COMMANDS USED
=============================================================================

1. All GTA selective codes:
   SELECT * FROM gta_selective_codes

2. Group distribution by region:
   SELECT region_macro, COUNT(*) FROM indigenous_groups 
   GROUP BY region_macro ORDER BY COUNT(*) DESC

3. Domain distribution:
   SELECT d.name_ja, d.name_en, COUNT(tid.tk_item_id) as item_count 
   FROM tk_domains d LEFT JOIN tk_item_domains tid ON d.id = tid.domain_id 
   GROUP BY d.id ORDER BY item_count DESC

4. Myth function assignment distribution:
   SELECT mfa.function_id, ft.name_ja, ft.name_en, COUNT(mfa.myth_id) 
   FROM myth_function_assignments mfa 
   JOIN function_taxonomy ft ON mfa.function_id = ft.id 
   GROUP BY mfa.function_id ORDER BY COUNT(*) DESC

5. Prophecy-themed myths:
   SELECT id, title, culture, region, description FROM myths 
   WHERE title LIKE '%prophecy%' OR title LIKE '%future%' 
   OR description LIKE '%prophecy%'
